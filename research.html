Exploratory Research

When we would begin a research phase, we turned to three exploratory research methods to better understand how people behave in disasters and why:

Methods Used

  • Secondary Research: A comprehensive reading of literature, both academic and popular related, to a host of topics related to disasters.
  • Guerrilla Research: Quick and dirty interviews with people about their experiences with disasters, or what they think they would do in a disaster.
  • Interviews: Detailed interviews, mostly over gchat with experts in the domain of disaster preparation and management.

From these we initialy learned that non-patient inflow might not be an issue in disaster situations. However, when we met with our client at their office and conducted a number of interviews and participatory exercises they insisted that this is indeed a problem for many of their clients. This puzzled us. Luckily, Teletracking put us in touch with these clients for remote interviews. It turns out, these clientâ€™s did indeed have an issue with non-patients turning up in disasters.

Generative Research and Design

Methods Used

From exploratory research we had to generate solutions that could help people and institutions in disasters. For this, we turned to a number of generative research methods:

  • Stakeholder Mapping: Who are the actors in a disaster situation and how do they interact with and rely on each-other?
  • How Might We?: Drafting ideas of how solutions to our problems could ideally work.
  • Crazy 8s: Generating 8 ideas to solve a specific problem in a period of 8 minutes. Repeat
  • Visioning: Collaborative sketching. This generated some of our wildest ideas
  • Storyboarding: Here we illustrated scenarios of some of our favored ideas from the above exercises in action. We then used these to test (Speed-dating) our ideas out with key stakeholders.
